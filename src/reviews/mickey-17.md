The Academy Award winning director Bong Joon Ho delivers yet another dark, gritty genre-mixing story. In his latest work, he yet again asks the audience existential questions surrounding social class structures, greed, environmental issues and interrogates complex moral situations, all the while presenting us with a satirical action packed script. Whether you're the type of viewer who likes to sit back and enjoy the on-screen drama, or you're someone who enjoys diving into over-analysing, Bong Joon Ho's movies are always a hit and *Mickey 17* is no exception.

In relation to Bong's other movies, *Mickey 17* is considered to be an absolute flop, but that by no stretch makes this a bad movie. It's a ballsy piece that is fitting for the times, featuring an over the top villain portrayed by Mark Ruffalo. Ruffalo's performance was borderline unwatchable, in the best possible way. His character, clearly based on Trump, displayed traits of all the worst leaders throughout history which made me realise that if humanity ever did set out on a multiplanetary mission it would absolutely be led by a leader like Kenneth Marshall.

Throughout the movie we can see Bong's fingerprints all over it. One of his calling cards is drawing attention to food consumption: how it differs across social classes and how animals are treated in the process. An artist like Bong always tries to make a statement or an impactful commentary, something he always manages to frame his story's around... more than what can be said for the majority of Hollywood’s cookie cutter movies.

However, this movie isn't without its shortcomings as one of the weakest entries in Bong Joon Ho's filmography. The screen time was certainly much longer than it needed to be, with the back story of Mickey taking up a large chunk of the first act while having very little relevance or justifiable payoff in the main storyline. Despite the long runtime, the movie missed the opportunity to dive deeper into the enormous implications of the technology enabling the storage and restoration of Mickey's whole consciousness. It never stopped to ask the question of what it would mean for humanity to better understand human consciousness, how that could change our take on ethics or what it would do to religion. Throughout the movie I was waiting for that to be explored with a satisfying payoff but the topic was largely ignored with the exception of a couple lines of dialogue.

On the plus side, I'd never seen Pattinson in anything other than *Twilight* so it was nice to see that he actually has some range and depth to him as an actor. He was a true standout in this movie, I hope to see more of him on the big screen soon.

3/5 - **worth watching**.
